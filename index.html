The Cambridge Five were a group of British spies who secretly worked for the Soviet Union during the early and middle years of the Cold War. They were recruited while students at the prestigious University of Cambridge in the 1930s and later entered some of the most important institutions in Britain, including intelligence agencies, the Foreign Office, and government departments. The group became one of the most successful espionage networks in modern history because they were not recruited through money or blackmail. Instead, they were motivated largely by ideology. During the 1930s, many young intellectuals became disillusioned with capitalism because of the Great Depression and feared the rise of fascism in countries such as Germany, Italy, and Spain. Some believed that communism offered a better future and saw the Soviet Union as the strongest force opposing fascism. After graduation, the spies carefully concealed their communist sympathies and built careers within the British government. Over several decades, they passed thousands of classified documents to Soviet intelligence. Their actions compromised Western intelligence operations, revealed military secrets, and damaged trust between Britain and the United States.
